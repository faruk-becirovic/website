1. Theories of Interaction

Interaction is a communication between a user and a system. There are at least two participants in the interaction, and both of them are complex. The interface must, therefore, be able to effectively translate between them to allow for the successful interaction.

Terms of interaction:

Goal
what is to be achieved
Domain
the area of work under study
Task
operations and actions necessary to fulfil the goal

1.1. Norman's Model of Interaction

Norman's model is the most influential model of interaction. In this model the user and system are visualised as islands, connected by two gulfs; gulf of execution and gulf of evaluation.

The gulf of execution is composed of:

  1. Establishing the goal (WHAT)
  2. Forming the intention (HOW)
  3. Specifying action sequence
  4. Executing the action

The gulf of evaluation is composed of:

  1. Perceiving the system state
  2. Interpreting the system state
  3. Evaluating the system state

Norman's model only considers the interface of the system, it concentrates on the user's viewpoint of the interaction.

1.2. Abowd and Beale Model of Interaction

Abowd and Beale model of interaction is an extension of the Norman's model that addresses the issue with Norman's model focusing exclusively on the user's viewpoint in the interaction.

Addressing the issue is achieved through the introduction of two additional components, beside system and user. Those are input and output.

1.3. Human Errors

There are two types of human errors:

Slips
characterised by the formulation of the correct action but failure to execute the action correctly. It may be addressed by the implementation of better interface.
Mistakes
characterised by wrong intentions, situation in which user is not capable of formulating the action correctly.

2. Needfinding

The process of finding and understanding user needs and system requirements is called needfinding.

2.1. Needfinding Questions

Main questions that should be answered during needfinding are:

  • What do users need?
  • What do users want?
  • Who are the users?
  • How are they doing it, now?
  • What is the context in which they are doing it?

2.2. Needfinding Methods

To correctly conduct needfinding and answer on all necessary questions it is necessary know and understand the users, their requirements and expectations. This often requires establishing different user classes based on different categories in users can be separated.

To be able to get to know the users several methods can be implemented:

  • Talking to users
    • Surveys
    • Interviews
  • Watch users
    • Observation session
    • Video recording
    • Work analysis
  • Imagining the users
    • Construct imaginary users

The needfinding methods are:

  1. Observations
  2. Interviews
  3. Surveys
  4. Personas

2.2.1. Observation

Observation is attempting to understand current user behaviour. This requires finding differences and similarities among users. It also requires observation of processes, how things are supposed to be done, and practices, how things are actually being done.

The observation can be:

Controlled
users are observed in controlled environment, which makes them easy to reproduce and analyse
Naturalistic
users are observed in their natural environment, which makes them more reliable and useful, but also difficult to replicate.

2.2.2. Interviews

Interviews rely on in-person conversation with users. This makes them time demanding and requiring depth of knowledge in the subject area by the interviewer. Interviews can be structured and unstructured. They can also be individual or in focus groups. However, it can sometimes be difficult for users to articulate their wishes, needs and requirements. It is also difficult to assemble a sample of users that is representative of the overall user population.

2.2.3. Surveys

Surveys are similar to interviews, but they address some drawbacks of the interviews. They tend to be more structured and thus more cost effective, requiring less time and effort. This makes surveys easier to conduct and capable of being conducted on a larger sample of users.

Survey results are also generally easier to compile, visualise and analyse. However they still require care when selecting a sample that represents user population.

Surveys can be conducted on paper, online or over the phone.

2.2.4. Personas

In absence of real users it can be useful to construct an imaginary person with a detailed description for the given role. This person is called persona. Personas should be constructed with care to make it a close representation of actual users that capture their needs and requirements.

3. Task Analysis

Task analysis is the study of the way people perform their activities. Task analysis aims to determine what people do, how they do it, what knowledge they must posses and what items they must utilise to fulfil a given task.

A task is a goal with an ordered set of actions.

Task analysis helps to establish user goals, and what actions are necessary to achieve these goals as well as how users are influenced by their physical environment.

Techniques used in task analysis are:

Task decomposition
Splitting tasks into sub-tasks and their ordering.
Knowledge-based techniques
Any information and instructions that users need to know, and how that knowledge is organised
Entity-relationship-based analysis
identify actors, objects, relationships and their actions
Ethnography
Observation of users’ behavior in the use context.
Protocol analysis
Observation and documentation of actions of the user. This is achieved by authenticating the user’s thinking. The user is made to think aloud so that the user’s mental logic can be understood.

3.1. Hierarchical Task Analysis

Hierarchical task analysis (HTA) is one method of task decomposition. Hierarchical task analysis is the process of disintegrating the task into sub-tasks that can be analysed using logical sequence execution.
