1. Basics of Python

Python is a dynamically typed, general-purpose programming language used in a variety of settings and projects. It can be used in various fields, such as desktop application development, data science, machine learning, as well as web-application development. It is often used in fields that require analysis and processing of large amounts of data, including biological data analysis. Due to its relative simplicity Python can be used by people with various backgorunds, that may not have experience in software development.

6. Basics of Biology

In the biology of the cell there are three main polymers that determine how cells function. These polymers are often studied using computational methods. The three polymers are DNA, RNA, and proteins. The DNA is considered to play informational role, in the sense that it stores the biological, or genetic information. The proteins are considered to play purely functional role in the cell. While RNA is considered to be both informational and functional elements in cells.

6.1. DNA

DNA stands for deoxyribonucleic accid. The DNA is a blueprint of life. The monomers of DNA are nucleotides. These nucleotides are Adenosine, Thymine, Cytosine, Guanine. They bind exclusively \(A-T\), \(G-C\). The entire string that describes nucleotides is called sequence. DNA contains functional properties of organisms. Not entire DNA condes for functional properties, some parts code to RNA and then to protein, and then some codes only into RNA.

6.2. RNA?

RNA stands for ribonucleic accid. The RNA is similar to DNA. The DNA transcribes into RNA, which is similar. DNA and RNA differ in their chemistry and their role in the cell. RNA monomers are Adenosine, Guanine, Uracil, Cytosine.

6.3. Proteins

Proteins are translated from RNA. They act as molecular "machines". Their buildsing blocks are amino accids. Proteins are linear polymers of amino accids. Evendough around 60 amino accids are typically present in the cell, only around 20 of them regularlly participate in the synthesis of proteins

6.3.1. Codons

Inside chromosome, a region of the DNA is transcribed into RNA. The messenger RNA is made into protein molecule in a process of translation. The RNA is made up of four nucleotides. And proteins are made up of 20 common amino acids. Because of this disparity every amino acid is specidfied by a combination of RNA nucleotides. These combinations are called codons. Codons are made up of three nucleotides and a codon can relate to only one amino acid. Since there are four nucleotides, and codons are made up of three nucleotides, there are \(64 = 4 \times 4 \times 4\) triplet nucleotide sequence that make up a codon. Every codon relates to at most one amino acid, however, some amino acids are specified by more than one codon. Some codons will indicate that a protein sequecne should stop.

7. Genetic Databases

Genome is constant. Genome codes the DNA.Genome sequencing should always produce the same result. Genome can be coding part, which contains genes, which can be transcribed into RNA, and then translated into proteins. Anoteher type of genome is non-coding part which contains repetative genome.

Transcriptome is variable. It can change between sequencing procedures and it depends on many factors. Transcriptome codes for the RNA. Transcriptome study reveals genes that are differently expressed between different cells. Transcriptomes, thus allow for disease identification, unveil specific treatment options, or enable additional research.

Protome is sequenced protein and Metabolome usually denotes sequences of monomers such as amino acids, nucleotides, or polysaccharids.

Types of molecular databases:

  • Primary databases:
    • Original submissions by experimentalists
    • Content is controlled and verified by the submitter
    • Examples include: GenBank, Trace, SRA, SNP, GEO…
  • Derivative databases:
    • They are derivated from primary databases, and are curated and often integrated with a primary database,
    • The content is controlled by the curator
    • NCBI Protein, RefSeq, UniGene.

Nonredundant, curated databases are:

  • DNA:
    • RefSeq(NCBI; semi-automated)
    • Ensembl gene index (automated)
  • Proteins:
    • RefSeq(NCBI; semiautomated)
    • TrEMBL(EMBL; automated)

RefSeq is contains a non-redundant set of genes and proteins for various organisms, and is generally considered most exhaustive database.

8. Genome Browsers

A genome browser is a type of browser that can retrieve genomic data. There are several different borwsers like USCS, Ensembl and more.

8.1. USCS

USCS

USCS is updated often, it is easy to use. It can be used to find a gene, by name or by sequence. It can be used to find gene structure, sequence orthologues, single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs). USCS posseses a gene sorter, that can sort the results according to expresion, homology… The data in the browser is organised into tracks, such as chromosome bands, gap locations, known genes, predicted genes, phenotypes, or even custom tracks.

8.2. BLAT

BLAT stands for BLAST Like Alignemnt Tool, it searches by indexing the entire genome, and works best with high-similarity matches.

8.3. Ensembl

Ensembl

Not as user friendy as USCS, however it is updated more often and contains the most recent genomic data. Ensembl genome browser can be searched by gene name, organism, chromosome, seqence within a cromosome, or by genetic polymorphisms. The data accessible on Ensembl includes genome assembly, variation, comparative genomics, gene anotation and regulation.

8.4. UniProt

UniProt

It is important to note that the Proteins are more than their sequence. That is the protein's function is determined by more factors than just their sequence. Some of these factors are localisation, function, structure etc. Uniprot is a unified database of many constituent databases such as Swiss-Prot, PIR, TERMBL, PDB… UniProt aims to provide high quality protein sequence database, with easy protein identification and thorough protein annotation. UniProt is to provide all encoded protein by a single gene of a species in one entry.

8.5. SNPs

SNP (snips) is a Single Nucleotide Polymorphism. They are differences in only one nucleotide. A typical human genome differs from the refference human genome at 4 to 5 million sites, which is about \(0.15%\). Most of those differences are SNPs. The SNPs can have a clinical significance.

9. Sequence Alignment

Sequence alignment is a method used in molecular biology and bioinformatics to identify similarities in DNA, RNA, or protein sequences. It allows researchers to identify conserved regions, understand the functional and structural characteristics of the sequences, and infer the evolutionary relationships between them. There are two main types of sequence alignment: global and local. Global alignment compares the entire length of two or more sequences and tries to align them in their entirety. Local alignment, on the other hand, compares only a part of the sequences and tries to find the best match between them.

Sequence alignment algorithms work by comparing the sequences letter by letter, and scoring the similarity between them based on the degree of match or mismatch. The score is usually based on the substitution matrix, which assigns a score to each possible pair of residues (amino acids or nucleotides). The substitution matrix takes into account the physical and chemical properties of the residues and assigns higher scores to the pairs that are more similar. The algorithms then try to maximize the score by aligning the residues that have the highest score and introducing gaps in the sequences wherever necessary to align the residues that do not match. The gap penalty is a score that is assigned to the gaps introduced in the alignment, and it is used to discourage the algorithms from introducing too many gaps in the alignment.

Pairwise alignment is a type of sequence alignment that compares two sequences and tries to align them in the best possible way. It is an important tool in various applications such as gene prediction, protein structure prediction, and evolutionary analysis. Pairwise alignment algorithms work in a similar way to other sequence alignment algorithms, but they only compare two sequences at a time. By comparing two sequences and identifying the similarities and differences between them, researchers can gain insights into the functional and evolutionary relationships between the sequences.

There are several algorithms available for sequence alignment, including dynamic programming algorithms such as Smith-Waterman and Needleman-Wunsch, and heuristic algorithms such as BLAST (Basic Local Alignment Search Tool). The choice of algorithm depends on the specific application and the type of sequences being aligned. BLAST is a popular heuristic algorithm that is fast and efficient, but it may not find all of the possible alignments.

Dynamic programming algorithms are a type of algorithm that solve problems by breaking them down into smaller subproblems, solving the subproblems, and then combining the solutions to the subproblems to get the solution to the original problem. Dynamic programming algorithms are generally more accurate than heuristic algorithms, but they can be slower and more computationally intensive.

Two popular dynamic programming algorithms for sequence alignment are Smith-Waterman and Needleman-Wunsch. Smith-Waterman is a local alignment algorithm, which means it compares only a part of the sequences and tries to find the best match between them. It is used to find the best possible alignment between two sequences with the highest score. Needleman-Wunsch is a global alignment algorithm, which means it compares the entire length of two sequences and tries to align them in their entirety. It is used to find the best possible alignment between two sequences with the highest overall score.

Heuristic algorithms are a type of algorithm that use a set of rules or guidelines to find a solution to a problem, but they do not guarantee the optimal solution. Heuristic algorithms are generally faster than dynamic programming algorithms, but they may not be as accurate.

BLAST (Basic Local Alignment Search Tool) is a popular heuristic algorithm for sequence alignment. It works by finding short, identical or similar sequences (called "words") in the query and subject sequences, and extending the alignment from these seeds. It is fast and efficient, but it may not find all of the possible alignments. There are several versions of BLAST available, including BLASTN for nucleotide-nucleotide alignment, BLASTP for protein-protein alignment, and BLASTX for protein-nucleotide alignment.

Aligning two DNA sequences to identify the conserved regions and infer the evolutionary relationships between them. For example, aligning the sequences of two related genes from different species can help researchers understand how the genes have evolved over time and how they are similar or different.

Aligning two protein sequences to predict the structure and function of the proteins. By aligning the sequences of two proteins that are known to have similar structures or functions, researchers can identify the conserved regions that are likely to be important for the protein's function.

Aligning two RNA sequences to identify the regions of complementarity and predict the secondary structure of the RNA molecules. By aligning the sequences of two RNA molecules that are known to interact with each other, researchers can identify the regions of complementarity that are likely to be involved in the interaction.

Aligning two sequences of different types (e.g., DNA and protein) to identify the regions of conservation and infer the evolutionary relationships between them. By aligning the sequences of a protein and its corresponding gene, researchers can understand how the protein is encoded by the gene and how it has evolved over time.
